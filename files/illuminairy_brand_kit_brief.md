# Illuminairy SAT — Brand Kit Design Brief

---

## 1. Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

**Who she is:** Suburban mom, age 42–50. Married. Household income $150K–$300K. She has a child who is a high school junior or senior (age 16–18) approaching college admissions.

She may also have an older child who's already in college or graduated — and that experience is often what makes her a better buyer. She watched her first kid go through the SAT process without tutoring, saw the score come back low, saw the college options narrow, and told herself "I'm not making that mistake again." She knows what's at stake because she's already lived through the consequences of not acting. She doesn't need to be convinced that SAT prep matters — she needs to find the right person to do it this time.

**Where she lives:** Suburban communities within 30–60 minutes of a major metro. Think Alpharetta, Dunwoody, Johns Creek (Atlanta); Cary/Apex (Raleigh); Naperville (Chicago); Plano/Frisco (Dallas); Chesterfield (St. Louis). Home value $400K–$1.1M, averaging $600–800K. Single-family, 4-5 bedroom, good school district.

**Her financial posture:** Not paycheck-to-paycheck. By the time her kid is 17, she and her partner have accumulated savings, retirement accounts, possibly equity from a home purchased 10–15 years ago. She's financially secure but not extravagant. She's willing to spend on her child's future — orthodontics, club sports, tutoring — but she expects quality and professionalism in return. She's not price-sensitive at $99/week if she trusts you. She IS trust-sensitive.

**Her car:** Lexus RX, BMW X3, Audi Q5, Toyota Highlander, Volvo XC60. Clean, reliable, premium-but-not-flashy.

**Her aesthetic world:** She shops at Anthropologie, West Elm, Pottery Barn. Her kitchen has a marble or quartz countertop. She follows interior design and travel accounts on Instagram. She reads real estate listings for fun. She notices typography and color even if she couldn't name what she's noticing. She responds to things that feel curated, intentional, and premium — not things that feel like they're trying too hard.

**Her digital behavior:** She scrolls Instagram and Facebook in the evening, 9–11pm, after the kids are settled. She texts more than she emails. She's comfortable with apps and online purchasing but she's not a digital native — she's GenX or elder Millennial. She does NOT respond to countdown timers, flashing urgency banners, or "ONLY 3 SPOTS LEFT" pressure tactics. She smells marketing from a mile away. She trusts professionals who demonstrate expertise quietly.

**Her emotional state when she encounters your ad:** Stressed, confused, slightly guilty. Her kid's SAT score just came back way lower than expected. She's been told her kid is smart — the GPA confirms it — so why is the SAT score so low? She's worried about college options narrowing. She's wondering if she waited too long. She's googling "why is my kid's SAT score so low" and "SAT tutoring near me" at 10pm. She wants someone to tell her: your kid isn't broken, here's what happened, here's how to fix it, and there's still time.

**What she does NOT want:** A tech platform. A chatbot. An algorithm. A startup vibe. A hard sell. She wants a human expert who understands her specific child's situation and can build a plan. She'll pay premium for that.

**Lifestyle & taste brands she resonates with:**
- Anthropologie (curated, warm, textured, artful but not pretentious)
- West Elm (clean, modern, warm materials, mid-century influence)
- Kinfolk Magazine (editorial, quiet luxury, restrained elegance)
- Aesop (premium, minimal, substance-over-flash)
- Jenni Kayne (California-modern, earthy, understated)
- The School of Life (intellectual but accessible, warm but serious)

## 2. Brand Personality

### Voice
Calm, sharp, and direct. High signal-to-noise ratio. We sound like the smartest person in the room who doesn't need to prove it. We explain complex things simply. We never hype. We never oversell. We lead with insight and let the parent arrive at the conclusion herself.

We're experts. We don't need to give guarantees — guarantees are for companies that aren't confident in their own work. We don't shout. We whisper. The confidence is quiet because it's real. We're a pleasure to work with — flexible, responsive, and human. We adapt to the family's schedule, their kid's learning style, their specific goals. We're not rigid or institutional. We're rigorous but warm.

### The Name: Illuminairy

The name is not random. It's the intersection of two carefully chosen ideas.

**Illuminate** — to supply or brighten something with light. Literally: to light up what was dark. Metaphorically: to make a complex concept easier to understand, to shed light on something that was previously confusing. "The professor's lecture helped illuminate the difficult text." That's what we do — we take a confusing SAT score and illuminate exactly what happened, why, and what to do about it. There's also a historical dimension: to illuminate a manuscript was to decorate it with gold, silver, and brilliant color — to elevate something ordinary into something exceptional. We take a student's raw potential and illuminate it.

**Luminary** — an inspiring, highly respected leader or expert in a specific field. A guiding light. Someone who metaphorically "shines" and enlightens others with their knowledge and achievements. A luminary is not a celebrity — a luminary is a person of substance. The word derives from the Latin *lumen*, meaning light. A luminary is also a celestial body that emits light — the sun, the moon, the stars.

**Illuminairy is both.** We illuminate — we make complex things clear, we shed light on what's actually happening with a student's performance. And we are luminaries — experts, guiding lights, people of substance in our field. The constellation wordmark ties this together: points of light (luminaries) connected by structure (illumination). The name carries our entire brand philosophy in two syllables.

There's one more layer to the metaphor: a path being lit. A guide lighting the way forward. The parent and student are on a journey from where they are (a score that came back too low) to where they need to be (a score that opens the doors they want open). The path exists — it's specific, it's structured, it's achievable. But right now it's dark. They can't see what to do, how long it takes, or whether they'll get there. We light the path. We walk alongside them as a guide, illuminating each next step until they reach their destination. That's not a tagline — that's literally what the service does, screen by screen in the funnel and week by week in the program.

### Language Rules

**We use customer voice.** We say things the way parents actually say them — not the way marketers write them. This is non-negotiable across every touchpoint: ads, funnel screens, emails, SMS, the website, the PDF report.

**We say:**
- "Get their score up" — not "boost their score" or "improve their performance"
- "Why they scored low" — not "identify areas of opportunity"
- "Get into their target schools" — not "maximize college admissions outcomes"
- "How long it takes" — not "optimal preparation timeline"
- "What went wrong" — not "diagnostic assessment results"
- "A plan for exactly what to do" — not "a personalized learning pathway"
- "Help them get their score up by October" — not "achieve measurable score gains within your target timeframe"

**We never use:**
- Marketer catchphrases ("better tutors, better results" / "unlock your potential" / "your journey starts here")
- Corporate education jargon ("learning outcomes" / "student success metrics" / "holistic approach")
- Hype language ("game-changing" / "revolutionary" / "transform your score")
- Vague superlatives ("the best" / "world-class" / "unmatched")

**The test:** If a mom wouldn't say it out loud to her friend at a soccer game, we don't write it. "I found someone who's going to help Jake get his score up before October" — that's how she'd describe us. Not "I enrolled Jake in a transformative SAT acceleration program." If our copy sounds like a brochure, it's wrong.

### Communication Style

Direct. Specific. Plain-spoken. Every word means exactly what it means — no translation required, no subtext, no spin.

We never use vague qualifiers or open-ended language that sounds like it says something but actually says nothing. If we can't be specific, we don't say it at all.

**Specific, not vague:**
- "A 200-point increase takes about 80 hours of focused prep" — not "significant score improvement is possible with dedicated effort"
- "Your child scored in the 1100s, they need a 1350 for UGA, that's a 200-point gap" — not "there's room for meaningful growth"
- "12 weeks, 3 sessions per week, 6 full-length practice tests" — not "a comprehensive preparation experience"
- "63% of students improve on a retake" — not "most students see positive results"
- "The October SAT is 18 weeks away" — not "time is running out"

**No glittering generalities.** A glittering generality is a phrase that sounds positive but communicates nothing: "We're passionate about helping students succeed." What does that mean? Nothing. Everyone says it. It's decoration. We don't decorate. We inform.

**Every sentence should pass this test:** Can the reader extract a specific fact, number, action, or insight from it? If the answer is no — if the sentence could appear on any tutoring company's website and still make sense — delete it.

### Personality Traits
- **Confident, not arrogant.** We know our stuff. We don't need to shout about it.
- **Warm, not soft.** We care about the kid. We're also direct about what it takes.
- **Premium, not exclusive.** This feels high-quality, not gatekeepy.
- **Youthful, not juvenile.** There's energy and modernity here, but it's refined.
- **Academic, not stuffy.** We take education seriously without being boring about it.
- **Geometric, not decorative.** Our visual language has structure, precision, and intention.

### Brand Tensions (Both/And, Not Either/Or)
- Soft colors AND harsh edges/lines
- Energetic AND refined
- Bold AND not overpowering
- Artsy AND data-driven
- Editorial AND functional
- Mid-century modern warmth AND mathematical precision
- Contrarian AND credible
- Intellectual AND accessible
- Research-backed AND never preachy

### Intellectual Identity

We are academic — but not in a high school or middle school way. Not in a fluorescent-lit classroom way. Not in a "your teacher put a poster of Einstein on the wall" way.

We're academic in a warm-professor's-Frank-Lloyd-Wright-home way. Wood panels. Floor-to-ceiling bookshelves. A conversation over coffee where someone explains something complex and it suddenly clicks. We're the professor who doesn't use slides — they just talk, and you lean forward because every sentence matters.

We are research-backed, innovative, and well-informed — but we carry that in an understated, assumed way. We don't plaster "SCIENCE-BASED" across everything. We cite College Board data mid-sentence like it's obvious, because to us it is. The research is woven into how we think, not stamped on as a badge.

We respect our audience's intelligence. Our core parent is a smart woman who can sniff out fluff instantly. She knows when she's being marketed to. She knows when credentials are hollow. She has her own ideals about education and she's skeptical of institutions that don't earn her trust. She respects strong, clear voices — people who say what they actually think, not what's safe. Think Jordan Peterson's intellectual confidence, Malcolm Gladwell's reframing of conventional wisdom, or Tim Ferriss's systematic deconstruction of complex problems. Not their politics — their posture. The willingness to say "the conventional approach is wrong, and here's specifically why."

She's discerning. Not conspiratorial — discerning. She doesn't blindly trust the news on either side. She doesn't blindly trust her kid's school. She doesn't blindly trust the College Board, or the tutoring industry, or any brand that leads with promises instead of proof. She forms her own conclusions from her own observations. When her kid comes home with a 95 average but can't break 1100 on the SAT, she doesn't assume her kid is dumb — she assumes something else is going on that nobody is explaining to her.

That's exactly why our approach is value-first, never pushy. We don't sell. We show her what we see. We explain why it happened. We lay out what it would take. And then we let her decide. The sale happens because the insight was so clearly correct that the next step is obvious — not because we pressured her into it. Our funnel is designed so that by the time she reaches the CTA, she's already thinking "okay, I need this." We earned that through education, not manipulation.

This is a woman who will research you before she buys. She'll google the company, check for reviews, look at the founder's LinkedIn. She needs to see substance, not polish. The brand has to hold up under scrutiny because she will scrutinize.

### How She Buys

She doesn't buy on impulse — but she's not a worrier either. She doesn't need to call five competitors, read 40 reviews, or ask her husband for permission. She trusts herself. She's confident, autonomous, and decisive. When she's evaluated something and it checks out, she moves. Quickly and without drama.

She acts quickly, but not impulsively. There's a difference. Impulsive buyers fall for countdown timers, "only 3 spots left" scarcity tactics, and high-pressure closes. She sees right through all of that. Fancy marketing tactics don't work on her — they actively repel her. She doesn't respond to urgency manufactured by a brand. She responds to urgency that exists in reality (her kid's test date is 12 weeks away — that's a real deadline, not a sales tactic). She responds to facts and outcomes. Show her the data, show her the logic, show her what's possible, and get out of her way.

Her buying process is short but conditional: give her a reason to trust you, and she's in. That reason isn't a testimonial carousel or a money-back guarantee badge — it's demonstrated expertise. If your analysis of her kid's score is clearly accurate, if your explanation of why they struggled makes immediate sense, if the math on the timeline checks out — she doesn't need a 14-email nurture sequence. She needs a link to book the call and she'll book it tonight.

This means our funnel doesn't need to be complex. It needs to be fast, smart, and convincing. We might close her in a few SMS messages after she completes the quiz. We might close her on the thank-you page. The mechanism doesn't matter — what matters is that by the time we ask, she's already decided. She's busy. She doesn't have time for a drawn-out sales process. She has time for "this is clearly right, let's go."

**Implication for design and UX:** Every screen should respect her time and intelligence. No filler screens. No "loading your results..." fake delays. No unnecessary steps. Every interaction earns its place by delivering value or collecting information she understands is necessary. The funnel should feel like a conversation with someone sharp — efficient, insightful, zero wasted words.

That's us. We're not afraid to say things like:

- "Your kid's 4.0 GPA doesn't mean what you think it means. Grade inflation has made high school grades nearly meaningless as a predictor of college readiness."
- "The SAT isn't testing intelligence. It's testing a completely different cognitive profile than school rewards — and most smart kids aren't trained for it."
- "A few weekends of Khan Academy and Bluebook practice tests isn't SAT prep. It's the illusion of SAT prep."
- "The average retake without changing your approach only gains 40 points. That's not improvement — that's statistical noise."

We say these things because they're true, because the research supports them, and because parents deserve honesty over comfort.

### Competitive Positioning (What We Are Not)

**We are not old-school tutoring.** Mathnasium has $18–20/hour teachers moonlighting who never even took the SAT themselves. Kumon is rote repetition designed for elementary math, not strategic test performance. These are commodity tutoring centers that happen to offer SAT prep as a line item. We are the opposite — SAT performance is our entire focus, and our methodology is built on score data, cognitive research, and structured improvement protocols.

**We are not a generic tutor marketplace.** Wyzant and Varsity Tutors will match your kid with a 70-year-old retired teacher who has no context for the Digital SAT, hasn't taken a timed practice test in decades, and teaches from a 2019 prep book. Our tutors scored 1450+ on the current Digital SAT. They know exactly what your kid is facing because they faced it recently.

**We are not a hype-driven edtech app.** We're not gamifying learning with streaks and badges. We're not promising "AI-powered personalized learning" that's really just a question bank with an algorithm. We're real humans building real plans for real students, informed by real data. The technology serves the methodology — not the other way around.

**We are the latest, most informed, research-grounded approach to SAT preparation.** We combine College Board performance data, cognitive science research on processing speed and test-taking, and structured tutoring from people who actually excel at this specific test. We are what a thoughtful, well-researched parent would build if she had the time and expertise to build it herself.

## 3. Visual Identity Direction

### Color Palette (Established)

| Name | Hex | Usage |
|------|-----|-------|

| Mustard | #dfc653 | Stars, ratings, warm emphasis |
| Pink | #e4abc4 | Tertiary accent, decorative highlights |

**Surface/background colors:**

| Name | Hex | Usage |
|------|-----|-------|
| Off-white | #f5ecd9 | Primary page/card background |
| Blush | #eee3e0 | Badge/pill backgrounds, secondary surfaces |
| Peach | #ecc8a4 | Avatar backgrounds, warm surface variant |
| Mint | #c8e2c4 | Improvement pill backgrounds, success surfaces |
| Lagoon | #90bdc1 | Cool surface variant, tertiary |

**Rules:**
- Flat color only. No gradients anywhere.
- Soft/warm surfaces paired with harsh/sharp edges and lines
- High contrast between surface warmth and structural precision

### Typography

**Direction:** Ultra-bold grotesk sans-serif. Tight tracking. High weight contrast between headlines and body. The typography should feel editorial and architectural — like a premium magazine or a museum catalog, not a SaaS product.

**Characteristics:**
- Headlines: Very heavy weight (800–900), tight letter-spacing, lowercase preferred
- Body: Regular weight (400–500), clean and readable
- Labels/metadata: Medium weight, tight uppercase tracking
- Numeric emphasis: Heavy weight, slightly condensed
- No rounded or "friendly" typefaces. Sharp terminals. Geometric construction.

**Suggested font direction (designer to finalize):**
- Primary: Neue Haas Grotesk Display / Satoshi / Switzer / General Sans
- Fallback free: Space Grotesk (already tested in brand explorations)
- The typeface should have a large weight range for contrast

### Geometry & Edges

**The core visual tension: soft colors + harsh geometry.** This is the brand's signature.

The SAT is a computational, mathematical, spatial, geometric exam. The visual system should reference this through:
- Sharp corners and crisp edges (not rounded consumer-SaaS corners)
- Clean grid structures
- Precise lines and rules
- Geometric shapes that reference math/spatial reasoning
- Angular compositions with intentional asymmetry
- Reduced border radius throughout (6–14px max on cards, 4–8px on inputs)

This creates a deliberate contrast: the warm, muted color palette makes you feel safe and premium, while the sharp geometric structure signals precision, rigor, and academic seriousness.

### Mid-Century Modern Influence

The overall aesthetic leans mid-century modern:
- Clean lines, no ornamentation
- Form follows function
- Warm materials (the color palette) + structural precision (the geometry)
- Think Eames, not IKEA. Dieter Rams, not Apple.
- Confident use of negative space
- Nothing is decorative for its own sake

## 4. Wordmark / Logo Direction

### Concept: Constellation + Light

"Illuminairy" = illumination + AI. The wordmark should play on the idea of a constellation — points of light connected by structure. This maps beautifully to the SAT (connecting dots, pattern recognition, structured thinking) and to the brand's role (illuminating what's going on with a student's score and lighting the path forward).

**Design direction for the wordmark:**
- The logotype should be set in the brand's primary heavy grotesk typeface, all lowercase: **illuminairy**
- An abstract mark/icon accompanies the wordmark, composed of dots (stars/points of light) arranged in a geometric constellation pattern
- The dots should feel precise and intentional, not random — they could form a subtle geometric shape (triangle, grid fragment, angular pattern) that references both constellations and SAT geometry
- One dot should be in the tomato (#f24822) accent color — the "spark" or "key insight" in the constellation
- Remaining dots in black or dark gray
- The constellation mark should work at very small sizes (favicon, app icon) and at large sizes (hero placement)
- The mark sits to the left of or above the wordmark
- "| SAT" divider can appear as a lighter-weight suffix when used in the SAT prep context

**What the wordmark should NOT be:**
- A literal lightbulb
- A graduation cap
- A brain
- Anything that looks like a generic edtech logo
- Overly complex or illustrative
- Rounded or bubbly

## 5. Icon System / Functional Graphics

### Purpose

Instead of abstract decorative shapes, the icon system should contain functional graphics that will actually be used across the product, funnel, ads, and reports. Every icon earns its place.

### Required Icons (designer to develop full set)

**Score & Progress:**
- Score improvement arrow (e.g., 1180 → 1450 trajectory)
- Progress graph / upward trend line
- Score gap indicator (current vs. target)
- Percentile position marker
- Before/after score comparison

**SAT Structure:**
- Math section icon
- Reading & Writing section icon
- Subsection/domain indicators (Algebra, Geometry/Trig, Information & Ideas, Standard English Conventions, etc.)
- Full test icon (all sections combined)
- Question type indicators

**Prep & Performance Concepts:**
- Pacing / running out of time (clock + speed concept)
- Stamina / endurance (full-length test concept)
- Focus / concentration
- Speed drills / timed practice
- Content gaps / knowledge gaps
- Pattern recognition

**Planning & Structure:**
- Personalized plan / roadmap graphic
- Weekly schedule / calendar structure
- Milestone markers
- Diagnostic / assessment
- Checkpoint / progress review
- Timeline (weeks remaining to test day)

**Style rules for icons:**
- Constructed from geometric primitives: lines, circles, triangles, rectangles
- Sharp edges, no rounded corners on icon strokes
- Single color per icon (pull from brand palette)
- Should work at 24px (inline) and 120px (hero/feature display)
- Flat, no fills — outline/stroke only at small sizes, filled geometric at large sizes
- The icon style should feel like it belongs in the same family as the constellation wordmark

## 7. What Success Looks Like

A parent scrolling Instagram at 10pm sees an ad. She taps through to the quiz funnel. The page loads instantly. Everything she sees feels premium, trustworthy, and specific to her situation — not like a generic tutoring ad. The colors are warm and calming. The typography is confident and precise. The geometric elements subtly signal "we understand the SAT at a structural level." Nothing is decorative. Everything communicates competence.

She thinks: "This feels different from the other SAT prep stuff I've seen. These people actually understand what's going on."

That feeling — "these people actually understand" — is the brand working.
