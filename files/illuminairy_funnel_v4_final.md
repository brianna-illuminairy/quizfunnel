### Screen 6: INTERSTITIAL — Why They Scored Low

*The biggest insight moment. Combines the GPA-SAT gap explanation with their specific diagnosis. This is where she thinks "these people actually understand my kid."*

**Section A: The GPA-SAT gap**
*Conditional: shows if GPA is 3.5+ AND SAT is below 1300.*

**"A [GPA] GPA with a [score range] SAT. We see this all the time."**

"It doesn't mean your child isn't smart — it usually means the opposite."

"School rewards depth, persistence, and revision. The SAT rewards speed, pattern recognition, and decision-making under time pressure. They're completely different skill sets."

"Smart kids often score low on the SAT precisely because they're thorough thinkers. They double-check, analyze deeply, and pursue precision. The SAT punishes that — 75 seconds per question, no time to second-guess."

*Research: "College Board data shows GPAs have risen for decades while SAT scores have declined. Nearly 60% of student grades don't match their standardized test performance. This gap is grade inflation, not low ability."*

---

**Section B: Their specific issues**
*Conditional based on Screen 5 selections. Multiple can show.*

**If pacing/time:**
"Pacing is the #1 reason smart students underperform. The SAT gives about 75 seconds per question — school tests give 3-5 minutes. It doesn't matter if you know how to answer them if you never get to answer them. Timed drills fix this."

**If focus/stamina:**
"The Digital SAT is 2 hours and 14 minutes. Most school tests are 45 minutes. If performance drops in the second half, that's a stamina problem — not a knowledge problem. Stamina is built through practice under real test conditions."

**If anxiety:**
"High-achieving, perfectionist students are the most likely to experience test anxiety — because they care deeply about getting things right. A 2024 study in the European Archives of Psychiatry confirmed this. The fix isn't relaxation techniques — it's repetition. When you've seen a question type 50 times, the anxiety goes away because you know exactly what to do."

**If content gaps:**
"Most students stuck in a score range have specific content gaps they don't know about — geometry and trig, grammar conventions, certain reading question types. School tests don't cover every topic every time. The SAT does. A diagnostic pinpoints exactly which topics are pulling the score down."

**If prep issues:**
"Students who retake without changing how they prep gain only 40 points on average. A few weekends of Khan Academy isn't the same as working with a tutor who can identify blind spots and hold them accountable. The approach matters more than the effort."

---

**Section C: Prep method reality check**
*Conditional based on Screen 4.*

**If Khan Academy / self-study:**
"Khan Academy is a great free resource — but it's self-directed. Your child is choosing what to study, setting their own pace, and has no one identifying blind spots or correcting mistakes in real time. That's why the score isn't moving."

**If prep book:**
"Prep books teach content but they can't diagnose why your child is getting questions wrong, adjust in real time, or build a plan around their specific weaknesses. A book doesn't know your kid."

**If went in cold:**
"Going in without prep means the score reflects raw ability without any SAT-specific training. That's actually encouraging — there's a lot of room to improve just by learning how the test works and practicing under timed conditions."

**If worked with a tutor (and score is still low):**
"If they worked with a tutor and the score didn't move, the issue may have been the tutor's approach, not your child's ability. Not all tutoring is the same — a tutor who scored 1450+ on the current Digital SAT and works through problems live with the student will produce very different results."

**If took a prep class:**
"Prep classes teach to the middle of the room. If your child's weaknesses are specific — geometry but not algebra, grammar but not reading — a class that covers everything equally isn't going to move the needle. They need a plan built around their gaps, not a generic curriculum."

*Callout at bottom:*
**"What we need to find out:"** We won't know exactly what's holding the score down until we see the score reports and run a diagnostic. It could be pacing. It could be specific content gaps. It could be stamina. Probably a combination. The point is that these are identifiable — and once we know what they are, we work on one thing at a time until it's fixed.

## LANGUAGE RULES

**Banned terms:**
- "Structured prep" / "structured preparation" / "focused prep"
- "Execution" / "execution issues"
- "Boost" / "improve" / "enhance" / "optimize" / "maximize"
- "Journey" / "transform" / "unlock" / "empower"
- "This isn't just our opinion"
- Any glittering generalities

**Use customer voice:**
- "Get their score up" not "improve their score"
- "What went wrong" not "diagnostic assessment"
- "A plan with weekly tutoring" not "structured preparation program"
- "Working with a tutor who keeps them on track" not "accountability framework"
- "Before their next test" not "within target timeframe"

**Communication style:**
- Direct and specific
- Every sentence contains a fact, number, action, or insight
- If it could appear on any tutoring company's website, delete it
- If a mom wouldn't say it to her friend at a soccer game, don't write it
